# The Mexican IFPA yield ban is a whole vertical, not a one-off pitch

> Two separate sources confirm Mexican law bars IFPAs (mini-banks / e-money institutions) from offering yield — which structurally forces every licensed Mexican fintech, including Bitso, to source yield from a non-IFPA like Etherfuse, turning a regulatory quirk into a repeatable, moated customer vertical.

Alfred Pay's meeting note states the positioning plainly: "Etherfuse is not an IFPA; acts as treasury/yield provider to IFPAs, which legally cannot offer yield themselves — strong positioning advantage in front of FAS partners" [shared/partners/alfred-pay-2026-04-22.md]. This is not a one-account nuance. In the BeKey call, Dave describes the same legal wall and names the marquee example: "one of the limitations of an IFPA is you can't offer yield… Bitso is an IFPA. And so they offer you virtual accounts, but they can't do yield. And that's why they use us. So in some ways, they're mutually exclusive" [shared/partners/bekey.md].

Connected across both sources, the pattern is a structural moat: any Mexican fintech that takes the IFPA/e-money route to offer virtual accounts and balances (Bitso, Alfred, and the long tail of neobanks) is *legally prohibited* from paying yield on those balances — so the yield engine must be outsourced to a regulated non-IFPA. Etherfuse, sitting on CNBV Resolution P090/2024 and CETES/MXNe rails, is exactly that engine [shared/partners/anchorage-one-pager.md]. This flips the usual sales motion: instead of competing with IFPAs, Etherfuse is the mandatory complement to them.

Why it matters for revenue and speed: (1) It converts a compliance constraint into a named target list — enumerate every Mexican IFPA license holder; each one is a captive yield-distribution partner that cannot self-supply. (2) It reframes the Alfred deal: Alfred's own instinct is to pursue an El Salvador/Bermuda issuance license to escape the constraint [shared/partners/alfred-pay-2026-04-22.md], but the faster path for both sides is Alfred running its MXN balance on Etherfuse collateral from day one (the exact All-Unity-style 'reuse MXNe, don't rebuild' logic) with a 50/50 yield split already agreed. (3) The revenue-share math is already templated at 10–60 bps of AUM by yield tier [shared/bd/partner-revenue-tiers.md], so onboarding a new IFPA is a pricing configuration, not a bespoke deal.

The wiki currently records Alfred only as a 'Won' deal with 'TBD' offering [wiki/entities/companies/alfred-pay.md] — burying the fact that Alfred is a live proof of a repeatable IFPA-yield vertical whose anchor reference customer (Bitso) is already using Etherfuse for the same reason.
