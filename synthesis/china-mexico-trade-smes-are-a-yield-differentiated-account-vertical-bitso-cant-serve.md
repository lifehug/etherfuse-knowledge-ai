# China–Mexico trade SMEs are a repeat-demand vertical where yield beats every IFPE competitor

> Two sources surface unmet demand for MXN business accounts from foreign (largely Chinese-trade) SMEs in Mexico, and Etherfuse's structural edge is precise: IFPE-licensed incumbents like Bitso legally cannot pay yield on those balances, while Etherfuse can.

In the BKEY/bmoni transcript, Natasha (bmoni's on-the-ground lead in Mexico) describes the exact pain: foreigners doing 'a lot of trade with China' and 'a lot of business-related payments' struggle to get a Mexican bank account, and want named virtual business accounts — something Bitso can only offer in the intermediary's name, not the client's [shared/partners/bekey.md]. Dave's own read on the market: to issue virtual accounts you must be an IFPE ('mini bank'), and 'one of the limitations of an IFPE is you can't offer yield… Bitso is an IFPE and so they offer you virtual accounts, but they can't do yield. And that's why they use us' [shared/partners/bekey.md].

That is the differentiator stated in the incumbent's own words: the licensed account providers cannot pay yield; Etherfuse's CETES/MXNe stack does. The bmoni onboarding note formalizes this as live work — 'Dave to investigate Mexico virtual business account structure (legal-representative model) for foreign SMEs' [shared/meetings/2026-04-15-bmoni-etherfuse-kyb-business-onboarding.md].

Why this matters for company goals: the China–Mexico trade corridor is a large, underserved, hard-to-bank segment, and Etherfuse's automated KYB + RAMP widget (launching end of April, positioned as 'the cheapest thing in Mexico') [shared/partners/bekey.md] is the acquisition wedge. Productizing a legal-representative account flow that lands foreign-SME balances into yield-bearing CETES turns a banking-access problem Etherfuse keeps hearing about into a distribution channel — with bmoni already asking to build it. This is a repeatable vertical, not a one-off, and it targets exactly the 'go where people aren't served' mandate.
