# AIFC ring-fence upgrades bankruptcy remoteness from two layers to three — and answers NIC's risk objection

> Etherfuse's global institutional sales guide is built on a Mexican+Swiss framework that explicitly cannot claim issuer-level bankruptcy remoteness under Swiss law, but AIFC-issued products gain a third, English-law issuer-level protection that is globally enforceable and is the mechanism to move Etherfuse assets from a B+ to an A credit rating.

The bankruptcy-remoteness communication guide — the standard script for customers, rating agencies, and institutional DD — is explicitly a **two-jurisdiction** framework: Mexican law provides the substantive issuer-level protection (asset separation action), while Swiss law covers **only** custodian insolvency. The guide flags this as a hard 'DO NOT SAY': *"Bankruptcy remote under Swiss law"* is prohibited because *"Swiss tokenization does not create issuer bankruptcy remoteness; it covers custodian bankruptcy only"* [shared/bd/bankruptcy-remoteness-communication-guide.md]. In other words, today the entire issuer-level protection rests on a **single** jurisdiction (Mexico).

The AIFC structure closes that single-point weakness. In the NIC NBK pitch Dave described the Kazakhstan structure as *"Dual legal protection: AIFC ring-fence (English law) + Swiss DLT registration"* giving *"dual-layer bankruptcy remoteness"* via an *"AIFC ring-fence + English law claim"* [shared/meetings/2026-05-26-nic-nbk.md]. Talgat confirmed the AIFC Court is *"constitutionally equal to Kazakhstan Supreme Court,"* has *"ruled against KZ government itself and won,"* is led by a former UK Supreme Court Chief Justice with decisions *"recognized by US Bar and globally,"* and has *"enforced judgment against a Chinese investor in mainland China"* — concluding that *"this legal clarity is the mechanism to get Etherfuse assets from B+ → A credit rating"* [shared/partners/2026-05-25-aifc-talgat-amanbayev.md].

**The synthesis no single source states:** for AIFC-issued Stablebonds, Etherfuse can legitimately claim a **three-layer** protection stack — Mexican-law asset separation, English-law issuer ring-fence, and Swiss custodian segregation — where the English-law layer supplies a **second, independent issuer-level remedy** that the current guide says does not exist under Swiss law. This directly rebuts the exact objection NIC NBK raised as its main pushback (single-counterparty/concentration risk vs. their traditional 10–15 global-bank setup) [shared/meetings/2026-05-26-nic-nbk.md], and it is the concrete lever behind the B+→A rating story that unlocks conservative institutional allocators.

**Actions:** (1) Add an AIFC/English-law addendum to the bankruptcy-remoteness guide for AIFC-issued products, since the current 'DO NOT SAY' rule understates protection once the ring-fence exists; (2) lead the NIC counterparty-risk follow-up (an open action item) with the three-layer framing rather than the single-Mexico defense; (3) fold the B+→A rating path into the AIFC SPC institutional wrapper pitch already being sold to NIC, Halyk, and Series-B customer-investors.
