# Etherfuse already has the multi-custodian answer to the institutional concentration-risk objection

> NIC NBK's single biggest pushback — that Etherfuse's reliance on one Mexican counterparty (BBVA) creates concentration risk versus a traditional multi-bank setup — is already answerable by the four-custodian CETES structure (BBVA, Actinver, Kuspit, Casa de Bolsa BASE) that the team itself omits in institutional sales conversations.

## The objection

In the May 2026 NIC NBK pitch, the sovereign-wealth fund of Kazakhstan's central bank raised the sharpest diligence challenge on record: "They questioned whether Etherfuse's single-counterparty model (BBVA in Mexico) creates concentration risk vs. traditional multi-bank setup" — they themselves hold Mexican sovereigns via 10–15 large global banks [shared/meetings/2026-05-26-nic-nbk.md]. Dave's rebuttal leaned entirely on the *advantages* of direct local access (T+1 settlement, local rates ~6.2% vs ETF ~4.5–5%) and he "offered to do deeper counterparty risk research for them" [shared/meetings/2026-05-26-nic-nbk.md] — implicitly conceding the single-counterparty framing rather than rejecting it.

## The answer Etherfuse already has

CETES is **not** custodied by BBVA alone. Four independent regulated Mexican custodians hold CETES collateral: BBVA, Casa de Bolsa BASE, Kuspit, and Actinver [shared/master-knowledge-base.md, wiki/concepts/custody.md]. The Maimone legal response repeats this to external counsel — "BBVA / Actinver / Kuspit" — as a multi-custodian fact of record [shared/partners/2026-06-01-maimone-associados-response.md], and the Canton Mexico deck lists all four as well [shared/partners/canton-mexico-infrastructure.md]. Actinver specifically is a Category I institution (no solvency problems) with a 15.23% capitalization index [wiki/entities/companies/actinver-casa-de-bolsa.md]. In other words, Etherfuse's CETES book is already diversified across four counterparties — the exact structure NIC said it wanted — yet that fact never surfaced in the meeting that turned on it.

## Why this is decision-useful

1. **Reframe the rebuttal.** The honest answer to NIC (and to any institutional allocator running the same diligence) is "CETES collateral is split across four regulated custodians — BBVA, Actinver, Kuspit, and BASE — so there is no single-counterparty exposure," not "single local partner but it's better." This converts a defensive concession into an outright win and removes the open "counterparty risk research" action item that currently stalls the NIC close.

2. **Tension with the consumer playbook to resolve, not copy.** The KTB collateral playbook explicitly instructs the team to *hide* custodians ("What NOT to Mention: ... Custody relationships or intermediaries") because for retail the asset's sovereign credit should carry the trust [shared/bd/collateral-playbook-ktb-krwq.md]. That logic is correct for retail but exactly backwards for institutional diligence — where multi-custodian segregation is the headline risk-mitigation feature institutions are screening for. The four-custodian CETES structure should be foregrounded in the institutional deck and DDQ responses (NIC's DDQ is an open action item [shared/meetings/2026-05-26-nic-nbk.md]).

3. **Roadmap implication.** New markets currently launch single-custodian (Korea = Shinhan only; Kazakhstan = Halyk Finance → Halyk Bank → Central Depository [shared/partners/2026-05-21-kazakhstan-partnerships.md]). NIC's objection signals that institutional-scale buyers will keep raising concentration risk on those single-custodian products — so the Mexico four-custodian model is the template to replicate (or at least to point to as proof Etherfuse can diversify) when courting sovereign and central-bank capital.
