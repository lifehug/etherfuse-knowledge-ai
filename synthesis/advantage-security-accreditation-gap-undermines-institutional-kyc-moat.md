# The Sovos-acquired NOM-151 provider is a latent crack under every institutional pitch

> Advantage Security's unconfirmed SE accreditation after its Sovos acquisition silently threatens the NOM-151 fecha cierta that gives legal weight to the customer agreements and KYC records Etherfuse markets as its institutional compliance moat — a near-zero-cost fix that de-risks the whole DD-led pipeline.

Etherfuse sells institutions on "compliance by design": its platform overview headlines "Built-In Compliance & KYB," KYC with "customer agreement generation — legally compliant PDFs with corporate data, legal representative information, notary details, and electronic signature capture" [shared/institutional-platform-overview.md]. The Shinhan AM C-level deal turned specifically on this — Heyryung Choi said she could "report to the government tomorrow that we picked up Etherfuse because they have this kind of information," naming FX/tax/transaction reporting as the unlock [shared/partners/2026-06-04-shinhan-asset-management-clevel.md]. KYC/KYB is mandatory for all participants and the gating step in onboarding [shared/master-knowledge-base.md].

The legal weight of those documents in Mexico does not come from Etherfuse — it comes from NOM-151 timestamps. NOM-151 is "the legal mechanism by which electronically stored documents — including KYC records — acquire a presumption of integrity and *fecha cierta*," requiring a *constancia de conservación* from an SE-accredited PSC [wiki/concepts/nom-151.md]. Etherfuse's chain runs Etherfuse → Mifiel (document retention subprocessor for KYC) → Advantage Security (the accredited PSC issuing the timestamp) [wiki/entities/companies/Mifiel.md, wiki/entities/companies/advantage-security.md].

The crack: Advantage Security has been acquired by Sovos, and "it is unclear whether the accreditation has been transferred, renewed, or is still valid under the new corporate structure" [wiki/entities/companies/advantage-security.md]. If that accreditation has lapsed, the fecha cierta of every Mifiel-executed KYC record and customer agreement is in question. That same "in administration" customer agreement is the legal bedrock of the bankruptcy-remoteness story Etherfuse publishes and hands to partners — the asset-separation action under Art. 70 LCM depends on identifiable, validly-executed agreements [shared/bd/website-asset-protection-rewrite.md].

Why it's actionable now and cheap: the entire risk reduces to one confirmation (does Advantage Security's SE accreditation survive the Sovos deal?) or, if not, one designation change (Mifiel points to an alternate accredited PSC). Either way it sits directly on the institutional-partner conversion path — the same path the DD-pack and CETES-custody-copy findings already flag — and closing it pre-empts a question that a Korean FSC, NBK, or BBVA-grade counterparty's lawyers will eventually ask. Secondary note: Sovos is a global tax-compliance software firm, and Etherfuse's named differentiator with institutions is multi-jurisdiction tax/transaction reporting [shared/institutional-platform-overview.md, shared/partners/2026-06-04-shinhan-asset-management-clevel.md] — the same vendor now controlling a Mexican compliance dependency is a plausible distribution/integration partner for the 50+ country roadmap, worth probing while the relationship is being clarified.
