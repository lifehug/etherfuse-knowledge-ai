# The IFPE license quietly unlocks Etherfuse's Mexico payment products

> Etherfuse's newly-kicked-off IFPE (electronic payments institution) authorization is the regulatory foundation that turns the virtual-account and fiat-rail products it is already selling in Mexico from partner-dependent workarounds into a fully owned, licensed payment business.

**The under-noticed move.** In May 2026 Etherfuse formally engaged BGBG to lead an **IFPE (Institución de Fondos de Pago Electrónico) authorization application** — a fixed-fee engagement of **$86,000 + VAT** to become a licensed electronic payments institution under Mexico's Fintech Law [wiki/entities/companies/BGBG.md]. This is a categorically different license from the CNBV resolution Etherfuse leans on today: P090/2024 only establishes that Stablebond *tokens* are not securities and require no authorization [shared/master-knowledge-base.md]. It says nothing about holding client fiat or operating payment accounts. An IFPE does exactly that.

**Why it matters: the products are already being sold ahead of the license.** Etherfuse is actively pitching fiat/account products in Mexico that an IFPE legitimizes:
- MoneyGram was told Mexico is the priority pilot specifically because **"virtual accounts are already live"** [shared/meetings/2026-05-13-moneygram-kian-schreiber.md].
- bmoni was offered **Mexican virtual business accounts for foreign SMEs** via a "legal-representative model," plus the automated KYB + RAMP widget for businesses to top up wallets with MXNe and CETES [shared/meetings/2026-04-15-bmoni-etherfuse-kyb-business-onboarding.md].
- Today the actual MXN fiat leg runs through a partner (STP for SPEI ramps) rather than Etherfuse's own license [shared/master-knowledge-base.md].

An IFPE lets Etherfuse hold electronic-money balances, run those virtual accounts, and operate SPEI in/out under its own authorization — removing the STP dependency and the "legal-representative model" workaround, and converting a series of bespoke partner arrangements into one licensed payment rail it controls.

**It also intersects the AVE peso-stablecoin window.** Mexico's emerging AVE stablecoin framework is already flagged as the regulatory event that lets BBVA launch its own peso product [referenced in the BBVA finding]. An IFPE is the natural licensed entity to issue or settle a regulated peso payment instrument — meaning the same license is a candidate vehicle for Etherfuse's own AVE-era peso play, not just a back-office compliance item.

**Decision-useful takeaways:**
1. The IFPE is cheap relative to what it unlocks ($86K fixed fee) and the timeline (BGBG estimates a multi-month process) should be tracked as a gating dependency for any Mexico virtual-account/payment commercial commitment (MoneyGram, bmoni, neobank savings plays).
2. Sales should know whether "virtual accounts already live" rests on a partner license or Etherfuse's own pending IFPE — the distinction is material in institutional DDQs.
3. BGBG, engaged here as a lower-cost alternative to Legal Paradox [wiki/entities/companies/BGBG.md], is now the owner of the single regulatory artifact that most expands Etherfuse's Mexico TAM beyond bond tokenization into payments.
