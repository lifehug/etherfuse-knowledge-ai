# AO is the proven SMB-lender reserve-yield vertical, not a one-off collateral study

> AO's wiki frames it as an experimental 'Baby West Bank collateral' use case to study, but a $200M-AUM US micro-credit merchant lender maps directly onto two products Etherfuse already ships with documented case studies — idle loan-reserve yield and yield-bearing collateral for lending — making AO a repeatable vertical play, gated only by the US Reg S restriction it currently ignores.

## The mis-scope

The AO page treats the relationship as an open-ended experiment: AO 'wants to use Baby West Bank for collateral' and the Etherfuse team 'flagged this as a use case to study,' with the collateral structure explicitly listed as an open question [wiki/entities/companies/ao-microcredit.md]. That framing understates a company that is a textbook match for Etherfuse's most-proven lender vertical: a US micro-credit platform for merchants with ~$200M AUM [wiki/entities/companies/ao-microcredit.md].

## AO maps onto two products already in production

**1. Reserve yield (the fast, obvious wedge).** Etherfuse's Case Study 1 is literally a digital SMB lender that turned idle loan reserves from ~$70K/yr in bank interest into ~$700K/yr by holding them in CETES/TESOURO — while keeping same-day liquidity for disbursements [shared/bd/generic-case-studies.md]. The Anchorage one-pager independently codifies both a 'Fintech Lender' persona (US-HQ'd Forbes Fintech 50, 4M Mexico customers, embedding CETES as a savings product) and a 'Microfinance Platform' persona (2M+ customers using CETES on idle capital between loan cycles) as *active* segments [shared/partners/anchorage-one-pager.md]. AO's $200M AUM sits squarely in this band.

**2. Yield-bearing collateral (AO's stated interest, but productized).** AO's actual ask — on-chain assets as lending collateral — is Case Study 5 (DeFi protocol adds sovereign yield as collateral, where posted Stablebonds earn 3.6–13% while backing loans) [shared/bd/generic-case-studies.md], and the Baubap treasury model where a lender holds CETES for yield, swaps to MXNe to disburse, and swaps back on repayment [shared/partners/baubap-integration.md]. The point: 'Baby West Bank collateral' does not need to be invented from scratch — Etherfuse has two shipped patterns for exactly this and should lead with them rather than 'studying' the request.

## The one real gate the page never mentions: US Reg S

AO is US-based serving US merchants, but Etherfuse's live products are Reg S — 'non-US persons only,' with the US restricted [shared/overview.md]. That is the actual blocker on a US lender holding Stablebonds directly, and it is missing entirely from the AO open-questions list [wiki/entities/companies/ao-microcredit.md]. So the sequencing is: for AO's US-domestic book, the deal rides the in-flight US-market unlock (the sub-$100K '40 Act / OCC path already being built); for any AO Mexico/LatAm merchant exposure, CETES/MXNe is available today via the Fintech-Lender persona precedent [shared/partners/anchorage-one-pager.md].

## Why this matters commercially

A $200M-AUM lender with an existing account and a warm CEO relationship (David Yao) [wiki/entities/companies/ao-microcredit.md] is an easy re-engagement, and reserve-yield is the lowest-friction entry that converts before any collateral engineering. Reframing AO from 'study a bespoke collateral idea' to 'run the SMB-lender reserve-yield playbook, then layer collateral' turns a stalled early-stage lead into a fast, repeatable vertical motion — and every lender behind it (Baubap, the Fintech-Lender persona) proves the template already closes.
